Understanding the Feral Cat Threat

üî¨ Feral Cat Impact on Biodiversity

Feral cats are one of the most serious threats to Australia's biodiversity. Since European settlers brought domestic cats, they have become an invasive species, killing over 2 billion native animals annually.

In Victoria, feral cats have led to numerous native species becoming endangered or extinct, disrupting the balance of local ecosystems. They not only directly prey on small mammals, birds, and reptiles, but also indirectly affect wildlife populations through disease transmission.

üìä Key Data and Research

According to recent research, a single feral cat can kill up to 740 native animals per year. In some areas of Victoria, feral cat density reaches 5-10 cats per square kilometer.

Our research team monitors feral cat activity through radio tracking and camera traps, discovering they particularly prefer forest edges and shrubland areas, which are often habitats for many endangered species.
